We don't breathe the same air as we used to

A potential IoT solution to air pollution

Posted by Anooj Shah on August 24, 2018

Introduction:

I intern at Celebal Technolgies under the direction of my mentor, Anupam Gupta, President. My project is called Smart Pollution Buddy. Air pollution is a major detriment to society and all future generations to come. It has changed our world and life from ever being the same as before. Many die from air pollution annually. It is a problem that must be prioritized. In order to slow down or even reverse the trend of air pollution so that future generations can see the same planet earth as us, we must monitor air pollution in ways never done before to point out the root of the problem. To battle this issue, I am introducing a smart city approach to fight the first step to cure this problem – monitoring. I will create a device that measures air pollution and shows the data in a way that plots the intensity of air pollution based on location. I hope this can create a map of the intensities of air pollution and can thus help fight the problem from the source. This device, at a large scale, will be able monitor air pollution and detect highly polluted areas which can then be analyzed to find the source of what is causing these areas to be highly polluted. This can be done through predictive analysis. After targeting the cause or source, efforts can be made to eliminate or reduce this cause. I will be gathering my information through global databases that have preserved this data of air pollution in specific countries. I will also be using open source libraries to aid me in the building process of this device.

STEP 1 – RESEARCH AND LEARNING:

Air pollution is something we are all affected by. Many believe it is a tragedy we must face with advancements in technology. The world is not the same as what it used to be, and it will continue to not be the same for future generations unless something is done. Now, there is major concern on the impact and toll on our lives due to air pollution. There are two types of air pollution – indoor and outdoor environment. Air pollution is defined as the emission of harmful substances to the atmosphere (Ritchie 2015). These pollutants include sulphur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), ozone (O3), particulate matter (small suspended particles of varying sizes), carbon monoxide (CO), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) (Ritchie, 2015). As evident, CO2 is not included in this list as it is not considered within this specific category and inside is viewed separately with other greenhouse gases. When these pollutants, as listed above, merge, the outcome is unpredictable and could be hazardous. For example, SO2 and No2, can react in the Earth’s atmosphere to form particulate matter (PM) compounds (Ritchie 2014). Air pollution thus has a multitude of negative impacts that not only human life pay a price for, but air pollution also damages ecosystems, and crops. Air pollution contributes to more than 20,000 deaths a year (Bakalar, 2017). The worst part is that this number keeps increasing and will continue to increase until greater action is taken. Researchers believe that for every day-to-day increase of 10 micrograms per cubic meter in fine particulate matter (PM 2.5), there was a 1.05 percent increase in deaths (Bakalar, 2017). In children, the toll from air pollution is even more significant. The Unicef Organization was able to measure using satellite data that about 90 percent of the world’s children, approximately 2 billion, live in places where the air pollution outside exceeds World Health Organization (WHO) limits (Carrington, 2016). This has contributed to 600,000 child deaths a year, which researchers believe are caused from malaria and HIV combined – both attributed to air pollution (Carrington, 2016). All countries must take some strides to mitigate the effects of air pollution. However, these 10 countries in Figure 1 show that China, United States, European Union, India, Russian Federation, Indonesia, Brazil, Japan, Canada, and Mexico are the top 10 emitters of global GHG emissions, and these countries alone produce around 70 percent of the global emissions (Ge, 2014). There are two ways to show emissions – cumulatively and annually. Cumulative emissions describe the total historic emissions of a country. This is used to see which countries are to blame for climate change. The start date is the one that varies but usually starts around when this data could have been measured. In Figure 2, one can see that almost half of the emissions come from just four countries: United States, China, European Union, and Russian Federation (Ge, 2014). This data of GHG Emissions has only been readily available since the 1990s. Before that, data was measured in CO2 emissions. As you can see in Figure 3, the five major emitters are United States, European Union, China, Russian Federation, and Japan, and they contribute a total of two-thirds of the world’s historic CO2 emissions, using around 37 percent of the global carbon budget (Ge, 2014). More action is truly necessary, and that is why I am creating a device to battle the first step of this issue – a better way of monitoring. People, in the east and west, all have something in common. This commonality is technology. We all carry around a piece of this technology in our pockets constantly. Thus, the easiest way to get live data is quite obvious. Smartphones are the answer. We can have a miniature air pollution sensor in our phone cases that monitors the location as well as the air pollution. This will slowly create a map of air pollution throughout the world. By knowing where the problem is coming from, efforts can be taken to stop the problem from the source.

STEP 5 – DOCUMENTATION OF FINAL PROJECT:



I have calibrated my sensor and tested the air quality outside and inside and it seems to be correct. I have checked with historic values in databases online and it is about the same. To mimic a highly polluted environment, I put some CO2 coming from a lit match stick next to the sensor and the levels spiked up. Another way to test this, is to just breathe in the box as we exhale CO2. This will temporarily show the levels increase. It is not possible to send this product to other places/countries so I used online data as test data to put into my database. ESTIMATED COST OF PROJECT
The cost of my product is about $45. However, I used only one sensor as this represents a minimal viable product. This cost is calculated with the inclusion of the main components such as the Arduino, MQ-135 sensor, and the wifi module. This cost does not include the less significant items such as wires/resistors, small LED lights, and the 3D print of the box. LEGAL CHALLENGES

STEP 6 – CONCLUSION:



We have a major problem in our world which has prevented our earth to be the same as how it was many years ago. This issue has caused increased health issues in many areas. This issue is air pollution. The Smart Pollution Buddy I have created will not fix pollution all together but will bring us one step closer to doing so. The device will be able to measure the levels of different hazardous gasses in the air and send this to a database which will collect data from all over the world. This will essentially create a God’s eye of air pollution. Just like how google maps measures traffic through satellite, we would be able to have a live map of the air pollution in our world. This data then can be analyzed through predictive maintenance to analyze the causes of the high pollution in certain areas. Then efforts can be made to reduce some of these contributors. This process will not happen overnight, but it will surely make a difference in the long run. GLOSSARY
Internet of Things IoT: This refers to the ever-growing network of physical objects that feature an IP address for internet connectivity, and the communication that occurs between these objects and other Internet-enabled devices and systems. Smart City: This is a municipality that uses information and communication technologies to increase operational efficiency, share information with the public and improve both the quality of government services and citizen welfare. Arduino: Arduino is an open-source platform used for building electronics projects. Arduino consists of both a physical programmable circuit board (often referred to as a microcontroller) and a piece of software, or IDE (Integrated Development Environment) that runs on your computer, used to write and upload computer code to the physical board. Big Data: Data sets that are so voluminous and complex that traditional data processing application software are inadequate to deal with them. Big data challenges include capturing data, data storage, data analysis, search, sharing, transfer, visualization, querying, updating and information privacy.
